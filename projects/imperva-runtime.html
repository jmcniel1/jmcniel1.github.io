Context

Imperva sits between the open internet and the applications, APIs, networks, and databases that keep large organizations running. Every request a customer sees has been measured, scored, and filtered through the surface my team designed.

I joined in 2012 as the Incapsula product was taking shape and stayed through the rebrand into Imperva, the move from a single WAF dashboard into a multi-product Cloud Security Console, and the eventual unification with on-prem data security under the Data Security Fabric. Three product eras, one continuous design problem.

The console

Security operators don't lack data. They lack a place to put it. The console had to scale from a single website behind a WAF to thousands of protected assets, billions of events a day, and a dozen attached products without becoming another wall of charts. The carousel below walks the brand and the surfaces it produced.

The thesis behind Attack Analytics

The earliest dashboards were honest but punishing. A WAF can fire millions of events a day. A list is the wrong container. Attack Analytics began as the answer: cluster related events into narratives, let severity and recency surface what's worth a human's attention, keep the raw evidence one click away for the SOC analyst who wants it.

That clustering thesis carried forward. The 2020 incident screen layered list, panel, and detail card so the operator could move from "what's happening" to "why" without changing context. By the 2022 Cloud Security Console, the same pattern was scaled across all attached products under a shared dark chrome.

Outcome

Imperva protects a meaningful share of the public internet. The console I helped shape is what security teams open at the start of an incident and what they trust at three in the morning. Over a decade, the surface moved from one product to many, from list to narrative, from monochrome WAF to a unified data and application security platform. The work is still in front of operators today.
